Optics

Diffraction

When waves pass around an object or through an aperture a phenomena called Diffraction occurs [1]. Diffraction is the act of the waves spreading through the aperture, or around the object. Significant diffraction occur when the size of the aperture or obstacle is of similar size to the wavelength of the incident wave.

Fourier and Maxwell

The way Fourier optics work is quite different than ordinary ray optics. A lot of us will have came across ray optics whilst we were at school, or maybe university. The reason for this is that ray optics is fairly simple to understand and visualise, this makes it perfect for giving us a grounding of how common light-based phenomena arise, such as refraction. However, ray theory does not describe phenomena such as interference and diffraction, so we need a new way of looking at these problems. The reason for this is that ray optics use approximate solutions to Maxwell's equations. These solutions are only valid when the light waves interact with objects whose dimensions are much larger than the wavelength of the light. To try and give us a more generalised understanding of a huge variety of optical phenomena, we see the optical field as an exact solution to Maxwell's equations. [3] This more general way of thinking about wave optics can accurately explain the operation of Fourier optics devices.

Fourier Optics

Fourier optics is a way of studying classical optics using the Fourier transform. Based on diffraction theory and the propagation of light, Fourier optics is a powerful tool that uses the Fourier transform to calculate properties of light. [2]

Scalar Diffraction

The study of diffraction, and propagation, of electromagnetic fields has a long history of development and there are a vast number of subtleties that need to be considered carefully to produce consistent and accurate results. In a lens with constant refractive index, or a medium like free space (a homogeous isotropic material) and in general, the field can be described by a scalar function representing either the electric or the magnetic field amplitude.
